Context to give

What Calmly is:

  • AI-first consumer dispute resolution platform — not a law firm, not a CMC
  • Consumers with similar disputes pool their positions; the platform produces an EV (Expected Value) report: the expected settlement value of a claim, given case strength, comparable precedent, and pool size
  • The EV report is independently endorsed before it goes anywhere near an insurer or defendant — you're not being asked to price on self-reported claim strength
  • Calmly earns 20% of gross settlement proceeds; no upfront cost to claimants or defendants
  • Architecture is CDR-complete — a systems engineering verification gate has been passed. This is not a concept

What you're asking Temple to do:

  • Write pool loss protection for the first claimant cohort: cover the risk that a claimant in the pool gets nothing
  • Contingent premium — charged from recovery proceeds, not upfront
  • Calmly pays Temple a marketplace access fee reflecting the origination value: a pre-assessed, independently endorsed portfolio with reduced adverse selection risk
  • Pilot size: 25–50 cases, housing deposit and marketplace disputes, £500–£10,000 average claim value

The ask

"I'm not looking for a policy today. I want to know whether Temple would be willing to look at the pilot case mix and EV distribution before we go live — and whether there's a structure that works for you to write the pool protection on that first cohort."
Specific and low-friction. You're asking for a scoping conversation, not a commitment.

The adverse selection argument

Deploy this clearly if they push on underwriting risk — it is the killer point.
"Traditional ATE has an adverse selection problem. Claimants only buy ATE on cases they think are strong — which means the insurer's book ends up skewed toward weak cases, because the people who know their case is weak don't buy. You can't tell which is which at the point of purchase. Calmly eliminates that. Every case in the pool has an independently endorsed EV before Temple writes the placement. You see the methodology, the EV distribution, and the full case mix before committing. That's a fundamentally better underwriting position than anything in your current book."

Objections and responses

"We don't write pools — we write individual ATE policies."
"The pool structure is the innovation — it gives you a diversified book at launch rather than individual case risk. The individual policy layer would sit within the pool structure; we can discuss how that works mechanically. I'm not asking you to abandon your existing policy form — I'm asking whether there's a structure that works."
"25 cases is too small to price."
"Agreed — which is exactly why we're calling it a pilot arrangement rather than a fully priced ongoing policy. The point is for Temple to see the actual case mix and EV distribution before committing to terms. The pilot is the pricing exercise."
"What happens to existing positions if Calmly fails?"
"We'd want to build a run-off provision into the pilot terms. The pool's existing positions would need to be managed out — Temple would hold the insurance relationship with claimants regardless of Calmly's position. It's a fair point and worth structuring properly from day one."
"We'd need to see the EV methodology."
"Of course — we can arrange a session with our methodology team. We're also in conversation with CEDR about independent endorsement of the methodology, which gives you external validation rather than just our word for it. I can share the CDR architecture document now if that helps you prepare."
"Who else are you talking to on the ATE side?"
"Temple is the first call. You specialise in boutique arrangements and make decisions without committee layers — that matters at this stage. I'm not running a competitive tender."
"What's the premium basis?"
"Contingent on recovery — same principle as Calmly's own fee. Temple earns on outcomes, not on cases filed. We'd expect to discuss a marketplace access fee on top, reflecting the adverse selection reduction we're bringing. I don't want to anchor a number before we've talked through the structure."

Before you call

  • Have the CDR package document open for reference
  • Have the cashflow/unit economics document ready — Temple may ask about recovery rates
  • Note that CEDR is "in conversation" — you can reference it, but do not overstate the status
  • Know the pilot cohort's expected EV range — if they ask for a number, have a defensible floor and ceiling ready
  • Review Temple's current ATE product lines on their website before calling so you know what to reference and what to contrast against
