Sensitivity analysis — at-scale model

The at-scale model's FMOS is most sensitive to average settlement value and platform fee rate. The cost floor is relatively stable (dominated by fixed module costs amortised across high volume). The key question is whether the market will support a fee rate of 10–25% of settlement — and whether average settlements cluster near £3,000 or closer to £1,500.

Model 1 insight

At-scale, the venture is viable. FMOS of 60.3% at the price ceiling (revised from 62.3% after HQ cost derivation) clears the 25% IVE gate by 35 percentage points — a wide margin that absorbs significant adverse movement in any single assumption without destroying the basic case. The architecture is sound. The HQ cost revision adds £13/claim to the cost floor but does not change the structural conclusion.

What the at-scale model cannot tell you is whether you can get there. The journey requires three stages of increasingly efficient capital, a track record that does not yet exist, and a portfolio of claims that takes time to build. That is Model 2's job.

The two models are separate by design. The at-scale satellite assumes securitisation capital at 8%, 38 modules at full utilisation, and all 10 IVE requirements operational. None of those conditions hold at launch. The scaling rocket describes a different economic reality — higher capital costs, lower volume, higher cost per claim, and sub-threshold FMOS — that is not a failure state but a necessary passage. Reading the at-scale model without the scaling model produces false confidence about what the early years look like.

Scaling architecture — the rocket stages

The temporary structures designed to reach orbit without running out of capital. Three stages, each with its own cost of capital, module count, FMOS, and working capital requirement. Each stage falls away once it has served its purpose. Stage 1 is about survival and proof-of-concept — not FMOS.

Stage economics — per-stage P&L

Each stage has different unit economics. Fixed costs do not amortise across high volume, so the cost per claim is higher than at scale. Cost of capital adds directly to the working capital burden on assessment org payments. FMOS is sub-threshold in Stage 1 — this is not a modelling failure, it is the reality of early-stage operations.

Stage 1 FMOS is deliberately sub-threshold. Stage 1 exists to build the portfolio track record that unlocks cheaper capital — not to generate a surplus. The venture accepts a period of sub-25% FMOS in Stage 1 because the alternative (refusing to launch until economics are at-scale) is structurally impossible: you cannot reach at-scale economics without going through the early stages. The loss period must be sized and funded explicitly. It is not a risk to be managed away — it is a cost to be budgeted.

Working capital requirement — per stage

The venture needs two kinds of capital at each stage: (1) operating capital to fund HQ overhead before claim revenue arrives; (2) working capital for the assessment org payment lag (paid before settlement recovery). The two requirements compound at Stage 1 because revenue cannot cover overhead at 1–2 module scale.

Capital structure note. The three stages are not three applications to the same type of investor — they are structurally different instruments. Stage 1 capital is risk-bearing (litigation funder — earns a return from the settlement spread). Stage 2 is debt-like (specialist lender — earns interest on a portfolio with a track record). Stage 3 is securitisation-grade (institutional investors — buy into a rated, diversified claims pool). Each stage has different investor requirements, different due diligence processes, and different timelines to close. Planning the capital raise for Stage 2 should begin during Stage 1, not at Gate 1.

The timeline is a planning input — it is the first thing to update with field data from the pilot module.
Model 2 insight

Stage 1 is not a small version of the at-scale model. It is a different economic structure with a different purpose: building the evidence that makes later, cheaper capital possible. The sub-threshold FMOS in Stage 1 is a feature of the design, not a bug. The question is not "how do we improve Stage 1 FMOS?" — it is "how do we size and fund the Stage 1 loss period explicitly so it does not threaten the venture's survival before the track record is built?"

The two models should be read as a single system. The at-scale model confirms the destination is worth reaching. The scaling model confirms the path is traversable — if the capital is sized correctly, the gate conditions are met in sequence, and the venture does not mistake Stage 1 survival economics for a permanent state.
